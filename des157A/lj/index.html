Game Design UX Best Practices

November 10th, 2021

The article “Game Design UX Best Practices by Amir Dori” discusses different UI/UX principles that can help improve Game Designs through 250+ lessons learned from games and proven data. Some key takeaways I learned and will consider implementing in the game project: UI positions, sliders, popups, draggable objects, and an intro video! In particular, the concepts from UI positions, sliders, and popups were very insightful.

UI positioning can help “comfort the ” hard-to-reach areas, depending on the orientation and placing interactive elements that can lead to monetization. Ideas that I never considered from the monetization of businesses are “Drak UX.” Some companies may use UX design principles that do not help the user. Instead, they use design principles to make the user experience increase the company’s revenue.

Sliders help expose many options without needing to navigate away from the main screen. The best practices for sliders include partly visible items visible on screen, so users will know there is more content. Also, animating from end to start upon launch by scroll-reverse can help the users know there is more content on the other side. I thought these two learnings for me were exciting and compelling.

Lastly, popups usually include a default exit button at the top right corner. This user interface design is prevalent and something I see across all methods. However, according to Dori, it is not the most effective way to inform the users. Instead, due to patterns of annoyance, if the content has value, design a button as a choice with the monetization element placed on the right side.

Overuse of Overlays: How to Avoid Misusing Lightboxes

October 23, 2021

Articulation of Thoughts

An overlay is a content box that is displayed at the top of a page and is smaller than the underlying page. Usually, the ackground is obscured which explains the term 'lightbox' to described the oerall emphasis of the effect.

Poor use of overlays can be frustrating for users, making an upleasent experence. The Nielsen Norman Group (NN/g) reminds us to think about the 5W's such as "Who, What, When, Where, and Why" to help determine if an overlay is appropaite for the design and when to implement the solution. The intention of overlays is to provide a way of quick access to important information. It is also for the designer's convenince, giving more features without making more features.

NN/g organized their article by targeting all of the W’s to understand when to use overlays in the most effective way. Designers should think about who my target audience is. For instance, designing a overlay function that is designed for desktops and placing it in a mobile application can be confusing and disorienting for the audience: think about the accessibilit and low-vision. Also, designers need to determine what action users will take and make sure the experience is quick and simple decision. Examples can include confirmation action, cancellations, or saving items. A challenge with using overlays is when and where to use it. Usually, designers should place the feature when users are less likely to interact with the task that is inline of the user’s sight. Lastly, designers should know why they are using an overlay. It is important to only use it when it is essential and urgent to collect small amounts of data! I really enjoyed the examples and case studies for each of these points, making it clear for me when building websites in the future.

20 Best Practices
for form Design

October 18th, 2021

Articulation of Thoughts

In the article, “20 best practices for form design,” the list structure of different points needed to optimize form design was very clear and easy to follow. Implementing these best practices and ideas helps improve the user experience. Some key takeaways that I learned from the article are column layouts, minimizing information, the order of information, and increasing accessibility. Minimizing input fields can help avoid unnecessary questions and only ask relevant information. Less information can help give a more pleasant experience for the user. Also, breaking down the information using the format, size inputs, the proximity of labels, and minimizing blocks of text can help reduce cognitive overload.

Feedback is also great for user experience, providing users a clear notice of whether they filled in inputs correctly or if their forms are submitted or need more changes, I find feedback forms helpful, as they instruct me what I need to do to complete the form. I also have an appreciation for minimal forms, as they are aesthetically pleasing and also give the most simple and direct experience for me.

Airbnb Form

The User Interface of Airbnb is a great example of forms: allowing people to book a place to stay and also creating trust between two strangers. During the initial search, the only fields you need to specify are choosing a destination users would like to go to, the duration of their stay, and the number of guests. The practice is friendly, simple, and streamlined.
